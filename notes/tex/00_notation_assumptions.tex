\chapter{Reading Rules, Conventions, And Standing Assumptions}
\label{chap:notation}

\section{Purpose Of These Notes}
\label{sec:notation-purpose}

Each chapter states its assumptions, defines its symbols before use, and derives
the central equations from a named structure: an action principle, a symplectic
form, a symmetry, or a balance law. The standard for every major derivation is
the same:
\[
\begin{array}{rcl}
\text{model} &:& \text{what physical idealization is being made?}\\
\text{space} &:& \text{where do the variables live?}\\
\text{principle} &:& \text{what variational, Hamiltonian, or balance statement is used?}\\
\text{calculation} &:& \text{which algebraic or analytic step produces the equation?}\\
\text{interpretation} &:& \text{what does the result say invariantly?}
\end{array}
\]
These five questions are the local checklist. They connect particles, rigid
bodies, celestial mechanics, elastic and deforming continua, and fluids.

Mechanics overloads its symbols. The letter \(\omega\) can mean a symplectic
form, an angular velocity, a vorticity, or a frequency vector, depending on the
chapter. The word ``action'' can mean a path functional, an action variable, or
a group action. The notes keep the conventional language but define it locally:
each important symbol is tied to a space, a convention, and a physical
interpretation.

The notes also distinguish three kinds of equality. A definition introduces a
symbol, as in \(p_i=\partial L/\partial\dot q^i\). A convention fixes a choice
that could have been made differently: the sign convention for the canonical
symplectic form on \(T^*Q\) is fixed by writing \(\omega=-\dd\Theta\). After that
choice has been made, the same displayed relation is used as an identity inside
that convention. An equation of motion holds only along physical trajectories,
as in \(\dot p_i=-\partial H/\partial q^i\). Many confusions in mechanics come
from silently moving an equation from one category to another.

There is a fourth reading habit of the same importance: keep track of the
stage of a calculation. Before a variational principle is imposed, \(q(t)\) is
an arbitrary admissible curve. After the first variation is set to zero, it is
a physical trajectory. Before a constraint is solved, a multiplier is an
unknown reaction force. After a reduction is performed, the same multiplier may
have disappeared from the reduced equation while still existing in the original
force balance. The text marks these stage changes explicitly.

Notation is introduced where it first does work. If a standard symbol is reused,
the local definition governs the current section or chapter. Upper/lower
indices in finite-dimensional mechanics label coordinates on a manifold and its
cotangent bundle; in continuum chapters, capital indices such as \(A,B\) label
material coordinates and lower-case indices such as \(i,j\) label spatial
coordinates.

\section{Notation And Conventions}
\label{sec:notation-conventions}

A convention fixes how formulas are written or oriented. It is not a modelling
assumption and does not restrict which motions, forces, or materials are being
studied. The standing assumptions in the next section state genuine restrictions
on regularity, endpoints, or invertibility.

\begin{convention}[Einstein summation]
Repeated upper/lower coordinate indices are summed unless stated otherwise:
\[
  p_i\dot q^i=\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_i\dot q^i.
\]
This is a notational convention. It abbreviates sums and keeps covariance and
contravariance visible; it does not add a physical hypothesis. In Euclidean
vector formulas, dots and cross products use the standard metric and
orientation unless a different metric or orientation is specified locally.
\end{convention}

\section{Standing Mathematical Assumptions}
\label{sec:standing-assumptions}

The assumptions below are different in kind from the conventions above. They
identify the regularity and nondegeneracy conditions under which the first
derivations are valid. Later chapters replace or refine them when a model
requires weaker smoothness, boundary work, constraints, singular Legendre
transforms, or continuum fields.

\begin{assumption}[Smoothness]
Unless explicitly stated otherwise, all functions, curves, vector fields, and
maps are assumed smooth enough for the differentiations and integrations being
performed. For most finite-dimensional chapters, \(C^2\) regularity is enough.
For continuum chapters, additional differentiability is assumed locally.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Finite-dimensional mechanics]
When the configuration space is a finite-dimensional manifold \(Q\), a motion is
a curve \(q:I\to Q\) on a time interval \(I=[t_0,t_1]\). The velocity
\(\dot q(t)\) lies in the tangent space \(T_{q(t)}Q\). Local coordinates are
written \(q^1,\ldots,q^n\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Endpoint variations]
For Hamilton's principle in its basic form, variations of the path satisfy
\(\delta q(t_0)=\delta q(t_1)=0\). Free-endpoint and boundary-force variants
are introduced when boundary work or transversality conditions are part of the
model.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Regular Lagrangians]
The Legendre transform from velocities to momenta is assumed locally invertible
whenever we pass from \(L(q,\dot q,t)\) to \(H(q,p,t)\). In coordinates this
means the matrix
\[
  \frac{\partial^2L}{\partial \dot q^i\partial \dot q^j}
\]
is nonsingular. This rank condition is intrinsic. Under a point change of
coordinates \(q=q(Q)\), velocities transform by
\(\dot q^i=(\partial q^i/\partial Q^a)\dot Q^a\), and the velocity Hessian
transforms by congruence,
\[
  W'_{ab}
  =
  \frac{\partial q^i}{\partial Q^a}
  W_{ij}
  \frac{\partial q^j}{\partial Q^b},
  \qquad
  W_{ij}=\frac{\partial^2L}{\partial\dot q^i\partial\dot q^j}.
\]
Since the Jacobian is invertible, \(W\) is nonsingular if and only if \(W'\)
is nonsingular. Singular Lagrangians lead to constrained Hamiltonian systems and
gauge theories. The systematic theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems was
developed by Bergmann (1949) and Dirac (1950). These notes name this boundary
of the regular theory but do not develop the Dirac-Bergmann algorithm; the
gauge and continuum chapters use constrained ideas only in settings where the
relevant reduction is stated explicitly.
\end{assumption}

\section{Units And Dimensions}
\label{sec:units-dimensions}

Dimensional consistency is a constraint, not a convention. In celestial
mechanics demos we often use astronomical units, years, and solar masses, so
\[
  G = 4\pi^2
\]
when the central mass is measured in solar masses. In rigid body mechanics,
moments of inertia have dimensions \(ML^2\), angular velocity has dimension
\(T^{-1}\), angular momentum has dimension \(ML^2T^{-1}\), and kinetic energy
has dimension \(ML^2T^{-2}\). Outside the celestial-mechanics simulations the
notes keep dimensional constants symbolic unless a nondimensional scale choice
is stated locally. This convention makes every displayed equation checkable by
units before it is interpreted dynamically.

\section{How To Read Derivations, Figures, And Laboratories}
\label{sec:how-to-read}

\subsection*{Figures}
\label{sec:read-figures}

Figures in the notes are schematic unless explicitly generated from a numerical
demo. Their purpose is to identify variables, spaces, and geometric relations.
For quantitative plots, the corresponding Python or Mathematica script should be
treated as part of the figure caption: the model assumptions and numerical
parameters matter.

\subsection*{Derivations}
\label{sec:read-long-derivations}

Read long derivations in mechanics on two levels. The first level is formal:
differentiate an action, apply a balance law, invert a Legendre
transform, or change coordinates. The second level is structural: identify the
conserved quantity, the reduced phase space, the constraint force, or the
connection hidden in the algebra. The notes spell out both layers whenever the
structural level is easy to miss.

To read each derivation, keep a small ledger:
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{input} & \text{output}\\
\hline
\text{model assumptions} & \text{equation of motion or constraint}\\
\text{symmetry} & \text{conserved quantity or reduced variable}\\
\text{constitutive law} & \text{closed continuum PDE}\\
\text{choice of frame} & \text{connection and curvature}
\end{array}
\]
Many mistakes in mechanics are ledger mistakes: using an equation outside the
assumptions that produced it, changing a frame without transforming the
components, or interpreting a reduced equation as if no reduction had occurred.

When a derivation contains a local coordinate calculation, the conclusion should
be read as coordinate-independent only after the geometric object has been
identified. For example, the symbols \(\Gamma^\ell{}_{ij}\), \(A_A\), and
\(\omega^a{}_b\) are coordinate or frame components of, respectively, a
connection, a principal connection, and torsion/curvature forms. Constraint
multipliers are slightly different: the scalars \(\lambda_a\) become a
reaction covector only after being paired with the constraint one-forms,
\(\lambda_a\,\dd\Phi^a\).

A useful test is to cover the displayed equations and summarize the argument in
sentences. When the prose summary breaks down, return to the model, space,
principle, and interpretation before trying more algebra. Long derivations in
these notes are meant to be reconstructible from those four pieces.

\subsection*{Laboratories}
\label{sec:chapter-end-laboratories}

The worked examples, exercises, and computational scripts are placed at the ends
of the chapters whose ideas they test. A laboratory is where a formalism proves
that it can survive contact with a specific model: a coordinate change, a
constraint force, a resonance, a pressure multiplier, a finite-time ejection
criterion, or a gauge holonomy.

Laboratories are checkpoints for the reading rule: state the model, name the
variables, derive the diagnostic, and interpret what the diagnostic measures.
For teaching, this gives three layers inside each example: local conventions
and assumptions, the derivation of the diagnostic, and the interpretation.
Stability, bifurcation, transport, reaction force, pressure projection,
ejection, and holonomy are interpretations only after the model and diagnostic
have been specified.

In laboratory sections, figures introduce the geometry before the algebra and
should be checked again after the final boxed result. A figure earns its place
by making a specific equation, reduction, boundary condition, or diagnostic
criterion visible.

\subsection*{Problem-To-Demo Conversion Rules}
\label{sec:lab-demo-rules}

Every computational laboratory in this repository should record the following
metadata:
\[
  \text{model}+\text{variables}+\text{parameters}
  +\text{units}+\text{invariants}+\text{numerical method}.
\]
For Hamiltonian demos, the minimum checks are
\[
  \Delta H(t),\qquad
  \Delta J(t) \text{ if angular momentum is conserved},\qquad
  \text{step-size sensitivity}.
\]
For probabilistic demos such as asteroid ejection, the output must also state
\[
  \text{sampling distribution},\quad
  \text{random seed},\quad
  \text{time horizon},\quad
  \text{ejection criterion}.
\]
These entries make a numerical picture into a reproducible mechanics
experiment. The same rule applies in reverse when reading a demo: the code
should reveal the equations it integrates, the units in which parameters are
measured, the events it declares, and the invariants it monitors. A plot closes
the argument after the model and diagnostics have been specified.
